Training Program

Four Core Modules

All videos produced by TMG Yachts — one of the most respected catamaran instruction channels. Watch in order. Each builds on the last.

ℹ️
Med mooring is the standard in Greek marinas. Module 4 is the most critical skill for this trip. Every marina we enter — Corfu, Paxos, Parga — uses stern-to mooring. Crew who don't understand their role during Med mooring create real risk for the boat and others.
1
Module 1 · Departure
Catamaran Manoeuvring & Leaving a Marina
Essential

Key Learnings

▶

Twin engines = full control. A catamaran with two engines can spin in place by putting one in forward and one in reverse. This is your primary maneuvering tool in tight marinas — not the steering wheel.

!

Wind dominates at low speed. A cat's large deck area means wind pushes the boat hard when moving slowly. Always factor wind direction before leaving a berth — it can push you into neighboring boats.

✓

Spring lines are your friend. A spring line held while engine power is applied lets you pivot the bow or stern clear of obstacles before fully departing.

▶

Communicate early, clearly, loudly. Helm must call out every action before it happens — "releasing bow line," "taking in fender." Crew silence is a warning sign.

Crew Duties — Leaving Marina

✓

Bow crew (2 people): Release lines on command only. Never release until helm confirms ready. Coil and stow lines immediately.

✓

Fender crew (1–2 people): Hold fenders in place until boat is fully clear. Stow fenders once underway — leaving them dragging is a seamanship fail.

2
Module 2 · Docking
How to Park Your Catamaran
Essential

Key Learnings

!

Arrive slow, always. Catamarans have almost no brakes. A burst of reverse is the only way to stop. Approach any berth or dock at the slowest possible speed you can still steer.

▶

Fenders go out before approach, not during. Once the captain starts the approach, all crew should already be positioned with fenders deployed on the correct side.

✓

First line ashore controls everything. Whichever line goes ashore first — typically a stern line — becomes the pivot. It doesn't get cleated hard; it's used to guide the boat in.

!

Never jump. Crew should step ashore, not leap. If you can't step, the boat isn't close enough yet. A missed jump injures crew and puts the boat out of control.

Crew Duties — Docking

✓

Before approach: Fenders deployed port and starboard. Lines coiled and ready. One person per stern cleat, one on bow.

✓

During approach: Silence unless you see danger. Don't shout instructions to the helm — one voice runs the boat. Only speak to report a hazard.

3
Module 3 · Anchoring
How to Anchor & Set Up a Bridle on a Catamaran
Anchor Skills

Key Learnings

▶

Catamarans need a bridle. Unlike a monohull, a cat's anchor chain exits from the center of the bow. Without a bridle, the boat snatches and shocks the chain. The bridle distributes load to both hulls and eliminates snatch.

▶

Scope ratio matters. In the Ionian bays (typically 5–15m depth) you want 5:1 to 7:1 scope — that's 5–7 meters of chain for every 1 meter of depth, plus freeboard. Check depth on the chartplotter before dropping.

✓

Set the anchor with reverse. Once chain is out, put both engines gently in reverse at idle to dig the anchor in. Increase to 1500 RPM and hold for 30 seconds. If the boat doesn't drag — you're set.

!

Watch your swinging room. A cat at anchor swings a wide arc. Check for other boats downwind and check depth around the full swing circle — Ionian bays have rocky edges.

Crew Duties — Anchoring

✓

Bow crew (1–2): Operate windlass, drop chain on helm command. Count chain markers aloud as they go out. Attach bridle before finalizing scope.

✓

Everyone else: Watch for swimmers, other anchored boats, and obstructions. Call out any concerns. Once anchored, deploy swim ladder and set anchor alarm on chartplotter.

Module 4 · Med Mooring
Mediterranean Mooring — Anchor or Mooring Block
Critical for Greece

Key Learnings

!

This is how every Greek marina works. Stern-to mooring (Mediterranean mooring) means you back into a berth with your stern against the quay while laying anchor or picking up a mooring line from ahead. Every crew member has a job. Confusion during this maneuver is the #1 cause of marina accidents.

▶

Two methods: anchor or lazy line. Some berths have mooring blocks with a lazy line (rope already on the seabed you pick up). Others require you to drop your own anchor as you reverse in. The captain will call which method. Be ready for both.

▶

The approach: bow in, then spin and reverse. Enter the fairway bow first, find your berth, then pivot 180° and reverse slowly toward the quay. Anchor drops (or lazy line picked up) as you reverse. Stern lines go ashore when close enough to step off.

✓

Lazy lines: pick up immediately. If there's a lazy line, the bow crew hooks it with a boathook the moment it's in reach. Don't wait — if you motor past it, recovering it is very hard. Attach to bow cleat and take up slack.

Crew Duties — Med Mooring

✓

Bow crew (2): One on windlass (anchor drop or lazy line pickup). One with boathook for lazy line. Listen to helm — drop/hook ONLY on command.

✓

Stern crew (2): One each side. Lines coiled and ready. Step ashore when captain gives the word, wrap the cleat, and take up tension. Hold the boat off the quay with a fender if closing too fast.

✓

Fender crew (1–2): Deploy fenders on stern corners before approach. Hold fenders against quay as boat backs in. Do not put hands or feet between boat and quay.

Trip Context

What to Expect in the Ionian

How the skills above apply specifically to our route — Corfu, Paxos, Sivota, Parga.

🌊
Ionian sailing conditions in June: Prevailing Maestral wind blows NW–SE, typically Force 2–4. Afternoon winds build and often die by evening. Mornings are calmer — best time to depart marinas. Water depth in anchorages ranges from 4–20m. Sand and weed bottom throughout most bays — good holding.
